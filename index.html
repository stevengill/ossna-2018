What are the Problems?

Steve: Late in 2016, we started talking to devs at Adobe to learn what the problems are with us not contributing back to OS.

Confusing process to contribute to OS

  • Email based process
  • Composed of Legal, Compliance, Patent, Branding
  • Took months to complete
Steve: Emails were easily lost in inboxes. Each team ran their own process, documented seperately from each other.

Employees avoided or ignored the process

  • Gave up on existing OS submissions
  • Legal became the boogyman
  • Started pushing to GitHub without approval (Big Risk)

Wild West on GitHub

  • 35 Adobe related GitHub Orgs
  • Repos abandoned, issues and PRs ignored
Steve: Without a central solution, the easiest way forward was for teams to create and manage their own org. We ended up with 35 Adobe related GitHub orgs. With no commitment to continue to support, many of those 35 orgs were abondanded without any notice. Sad to see issues and pull requests ignored for years. No central authority to provide guidelines around archiving these repos.

Adobe's Open Source Office

Steve: To deal with these probelms, we formed the OSO at the end of 2016 with the goals of improving the open source culture at Adobe. It was a grass roots type effort, with us only working on it part time. The original members were essentially volunteers as they responsibilities part of their day jobs. But the thing that brought them together was a shared love of open source and their desire to have Adobe to give back to the community.

Grow Open Source at Adobe

Steve: The OSO had one clear goal, that was to grow open source at Adobe. Help teams open source existing projects and contribute back to community OS projects.
Starter repo
Steve: One of the first things we did was a starter-repo. This contained the necessesary files for open source projects which you can see in the image. Code of conduct was based on the popular Contributor Covenant code of conduct. We give out sample contirbuting.md and issue and pull request templates. Our main license we recommendis Apache V2, but as you can see we also have the MIT license for docs and sample code.

Revamped OS Submission Process

Steve: The next obvious thing to tackle was the submission process! So we started to gather all of the requirements from all of the various teams involved in the OS process. We took those requirements and created a checklist for teams to follow.
Steve: To manage the process, we created a git repo on our internal github. We put the checklist of all of the requirements into a issue_template. Every submitter was required to create an issue in our repo and start filling out the checklist in the issue template. One big benifit of this new process meant that it was open and transparent. Anyone could view previous submissions and see how they went. We wanted to remove the mystrey behind the approvals. Over time and many discussions with various stakeholders, the list grew to 53 items. The process was still daunting and a little confusing. Our plan was for the checklist to be a first pass. It was quick to get up and it allowed us to build relationships with the stakeholders. It gave us at the open source office to learn the nuances of contributing to and open sourcing code. So earlier this year, from our learnings, we launched the new open source submission form. (Hand it off to Fil to talk about that)
Fil: The new submission form is radically simplier. We decided to remove the ambigous questions for more simple and direct ones. Easier to understand for developers instead of lawyers. We created categorizes of open source projects that the open source office would be able to approve without going through all of the stakeholders. Users loged in with their enterprise Git account. Handbook

Fil: Essentially: any questions that tend to come up from newcomers, we make a note of it and see if the info can fit into the handbook. In this way it organically grows and becomes more useful over time. Opensource.google.com/docs is a huge inspiration for this. It is gold standard. Today, our handbook contains centralized releasing documentation(npm, docker, maven central, chef). Info about how we manage github.com/adobe and how to get access. Also cotains info about our CLA The handbook started out with a focus open source, but has been bleeding into a generic engineering handbook. Goal is to open source it and hand it out to new hires as part of onboarding

Open Development Template

Fil: The intent is to answer the question: “how can I create a project that is open to newcomers, easy to involve yourself in, and scales as it grows in size?” Main areas, with a focus on answering why and how to document: Mission: what are the goals of the project? Licensing: what is it, what kinds exist out there? Governance: how do we add people to the project? Who has permissions in the project? What are the social rituals in the project? Process: how does the project get work done? Developed internally to help teams grow c ross-organizational projects that involve people from all across the company. It is a grass-roots effort to subvert the typical corporate hierarchical organization form and its limitations. We are dogfooding this process within the office, in order to make it more open and inviting for anyone to jump in and help out: 2 week sprints, everything in the open Added to our starter repo for new projects to get our suggested governance model
